## Citation
Manufacturing Supply Chains and Imports in the ADHD Drug Shortage. JAMA Health Forum. 2024;5(6):e241653. doi:10.1001/jamahealthforum.2024.1653

## Research Question
What are the likely causes of the attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) medication shortage of 2022-2023?

## Methods
This economic evaluation used time series data (2015-2025) from multiple sources including Symphony Health and the DEA's ARCOS summary reports. The study characterized US production, consumption, and trade of amphetamine-based and other stimulants, including manufacturer-level production volumes before and during the shortage period.

## Results
The sharp production cutbacks across several manufacturers in late 2022 and early 2023 coincided with a steep contraction in US imports of raw amphetamines (36.7% decline in 2022) and more modest declines in phenylacetone, a key precursor. The study found that US imports of amphetamines have been growing since 2014, with Germany accounting for more than 85% of US imports.

## Discussion
The patterns align with manufacturers' reports to the FDA citing a shortage of the active ingredient as the cause of backorders. The study reframes the discussion of ADHD medication shortages beyond DEA quotas, highlighting the vulnerability of US pharmaceutical manufacturing to international supply chain disruptions.

## Conclusion
The most recent ADHD drug shortage appears to be associated with a disruption in the sourcing of raw ingredients from abroad, particularly from Germany. Policies that strengthen supply chain resilience and require detailed information on API suppliers could help prevent future shortages.

# Article Summary 2
## Citation
Bachhuber MA, Jordan AE, Piper BJ. Prescription stimulant consumption in the United States compared with 35 other countries. Health Aff (Millwood). 2024;43(7):965-972. doi:10.1377/hltaff.2024.00323

## Research Question
How does prescription stimulant consumption in the United States compare with 35 other countries, and what are the implications of these differences?

## Methods
This cross-sectional study analyzed 2024 stimulant consumption data from the International Narcotics Control Board, reported as defined daily doses for statistical purposes (S-DDD) per 1000 population per day. The United States was compared with 35 Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development members.

## Results
The United States comprised 26.8% of the population and consumed 67.6% of prescription stimulants. Per 1000 population per day, the United States had the second highest prescription stimulant consumption after Iceland. The volume of prescription stimulants consumed in the United States was 569% of the mean of comparison countries. Only 31.2% of consumed prescription stimulants was methylphenidate in the United States, compared with 88.8% in comparison countries.

## Discussion
The United States remains a global outlier in stimulant prescribing, particularly in the consumption of amphetamines versus methylphenidate. This practice stands in stark contrast to Europe, where many countries have restrictions on stimulant prescribing to adults. The study suggests a need to better understand any differential addictive potential of prescription stimulant regimens.

## Conclusion
Additional research is needed on the clinical and public health consequences of these major differences in controlled substance policy and clinical practice between the United States and other countries. Policymakers should consider initiatives to promote safe and judicious stimulant prescribing.

# Article Summary 3
## Citation
Swanson CM, Wolfe JH, Calarge CA. Stimulant Medications and Bone Health. Curr Osteoporos Rep. 2026;24(1):1-12. doi:10.1007/s11899-025-00650-1

## Research Question
What is the potential impact of stimulant medications on bone health, and how can their safe use be optimized?

## Methods
This review summarized the potential impact of stimulant medications on bone health by examining psychostimulant pharmacology, SNS-regulation of bone metabolism, and data on how these medications influence bone mineral density (BMD) and fracture risk in ADHD. The review included 80 articles published between 1995 and 2025.

## Results
Stimulant medications can impair bone health through inadequate nutrient/calorie intake and stimulation of the sympathetic nervous system, which regulates bone metabolism. However, their use decreases fracture risk in younger populations (25 years old) with ADHD, likely through reductions in impulsivity and injury. Special care may be needed when fractures occur while using these medications, as recent data suggest psychostimulants are associated with impaired fracture healing.

## Discussion
The ultimate effect of stimulant medications on bone health may be a balance between effective behavioral modification and adverse effects on bone metabolism related to nutrition and sympathetic activation. Future research should investigate how psychostimulants affect acquisition of peak bone mass, stability of bone mineral density (BMD) when used through mid/late-life, and fracture healing.

## Conclusion
BMD is often, but not always, lower in children, adolescents and adults that use stimulant medication for ADHD. Despite apparent detrimental effects on bone metabolism/density, current stimulant medication use reduces fracture risk in most studies, when used in children/adolescents with ADHD for at least 6 months, likely due to behavioral modification.

# Article Summary 4
## Citation
Suetani S, Scott J, Hull J. Pharmacological management of attention deficit hyperactivity disorder in adults. Aust Prescr. 2025;48(6):183-188. doi:10.18773/austprescr.2025.066

## Research Question
What are the best practices for pharmacological management of ADHD in adults, including the use of psychostimulants and non-psychostimulants?

## Methods
This article reviewed the pharmacological treatments for adults with ADHD, including psychostimulants (methylphenidate and amphetamines) and non-psychostimulants (atomoxetine, guanfacine, and clonidine). The review also discussed the eligibility for subsidy under the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme in Australia, monitoring for adverse effects, and general principles of prescribing.

## Results
Psychostimulants, particularly amphetamines, have the largest effect in reducing the severity of ADHD core symptoms at 12 weeks. Atomoxetine shows a similar small to moderate effect compared with placebo but is less well tolerated. The first-line pharmacological treatment for adults with ADHD is a psychostimulant. If psychostimulants are contraindicated, not tolerated, or if the patient prefers, a non-psychostimulant can be prescribed.

## Discussion
Pharmacological management for adults with ADHD requires a nuanced, patient-centred approach grounded in comprehensive assessment and ongoing monitoring for treatment response and adverse effects. Common adverse effects include cardiac symptoms, appetite suppression, psychiatric symptoms, and sleep disturbance. Misuse and diversion of ADHD drugs, especially psychostimulants, are common and should be monitored.

## Conclusion
When pharmacological management of ADHD is indicated in an adult, psychostimulants, including methylphenidate and amphetamines, are first line. Atomoxetine is an option if psychostimulants cannot be used or tolerated. Pharmacological management requires ongoing monitoring for treatment response and adverse effects.

# Article Summary 5
## Citation
Singer D, Kothari PM. Maintained Symptom Control Following a Shortage-Driven Switch to Immediate-Release Dextroamphetamine: A Case Report. Cureus. 2024;16(7):e49876. doi:10.7759/cureus.49876

## Research Question
Can immediate-release dextroamphetamine sulfate be a viable alternative stimulant to address the ADHD medication shortage?

## Methods
This case report described a 30-year-old female who was unable to obtain lisdexamfetamine amid the shortage, found mixed amphetamine salts ineffective, but found success with immediate-release dextroamphetamine sulfate. The report documented the patient's medication trials and symptom control over time.

## Results
The patient initially had success with lisdexamfetamine and mixed amphetamine salts but was forced to switch medications due to the shortage. She found mixed amphetamine salts ineffective and experienced episodes of increased sensitivity to stressors, anger, frustration, impatience, tachycardia, and diaphoresis. She was then switched to immediate-release dextroamphetamine sulfate, which provided adequate symptom control and maintained symptom reduction.

## Discussion
Immediate-release dextroamphetamine sulfate may be a viable alternative stimulant to address the shortage. However, more research must be conducted to confirm these findings. The main difference between mixed amphetamine salts and dextroamphetamine lies in the amphetamine salt concentrations, with dextroamphetamine being more potent dopaminergically.

## Conclusion
The stimulant shortage has significantly limited the medication options for treating ADHD. In this case, immediate-release dextroamphetamine sulfate was a viable option to substitute other amphetamine-based medications and specifically mixed amphetamine salts during the shortage. However, more research is needed before a conclusion can be drawn about the practical viability of this switch.
